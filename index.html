Section 01

Getting here.

Three ways travelers usually arrive — and the same one piece of advice for all of them: do not take a taxi from the terminal touts. Open Uber or Cabify and pay with your Ramp card. We've tested it. It works.

EZE · International

Ezeiza Intl. Airport

≈ 50 minutes to Palermo

You'll come through international arrivals. Walk past the people offering taxis — they overcharge tourists. Open Uber or Cabify from the terminal and request the ride there.

⚠️ Skip the taxi touts. Request the ride from inside the terminal — the fare is shown upfront and Ramp pays directly.
AEP · Domestic

Jorge Newbery (Aeroparque)

≈ 10–15 minutes to Palermo

Aeroparque is right in the city, so your ride to the hotel is short and cheap. Same playbook: Uber or Cabify directly from the terminal. Ramp card on file.

Buquebus · Ferry

Buquebus Terminal

≈ 20–25 minutes to Palermo

Coming in from Montevideo? You'll dock in Puerto Madero. Same rules apply — Uber or Cabify, paid with Ramp, no reimbursement paperwork needed.

Both airports have free open WiFi — no password needed. Connect as soon as you land, request your Uber, and you're out the door before the taxi guys finish their first sales pitch.

Ramp doesn't talk to Mercado Pago (Argentina's local digital wallet). Add your card to Google Pay or Apple Pay before you land — it makes everything smoother once you're here.

Section 08 · A guide by Vicky Ciabattini

A Sunday in Buenos Aires.

Buenos Aires isn't a city you visit. It's a city you walk, smell, look up at. The point isn't to tick attractions off a list — it's to let a block you weren't expecting stop you in your tracks. This guide is built for that.

Practical note: you're staying in Palermo. That's a great starting point — one of the highest densities of beautiful things per square block in the city. But Buenos Aires has much more. To move between neighborhoods, just open Uber or Cabify — both work perfectly here with international cards, and most rides between the neighborhoods I'll recommend take only 10–20 minutes. The subway (subte) is also efficient on some lines; you need a SUBE card, sold at any kiosk.

— Neighborhoods worth your time

Palermo

What you have around you — and shouldn't miss. Almost everything below is walkable from either hotel.

Bookstores that are entire worlds
Bookstore · Palermo Soho

Eterna Cadencia

Honduras 5574 Sat 10 AM – 10 PM · Sun 12 – 8 PM

Not just a bookstore — a publishing house with a garden patio where people actually read. Curated mix of contemporary Argentine narrative, essay, poetry, and translations you won't find anywhere else. Order a cortado, browse the "House Recommendations" shelf, and you'll leave an hour later with three books under your arm. Mid-morning or mid-afternoon is best. No reservation needed.

Bookstore · Palermo Soho

Libros del Pasaje

Thames 1762 Sat 10 AM – 9 PM · Sun 12 – 8 PM

A beautiful old townhouse with books from floor to ceiling, mixing Argentine indie publishers with very well-chosen imports. They often host book launches on weekends. The back café has gorgeous natural light. Pair it with Eterna Cadencia — they're a 10-minute walk apart.

Cafés that justify the visit, not just the coffee
Specialty café

LAB Café

Humboldt 1542 Sat & Sun · ≈ 8:30 AM – 7 PM

Specialty coffee done seriously. Not the photogenic latte-art place — this is where the barista knows the farm and how each extraction method affects the cup. Small, quiet, good music. Order a pour-over or V60 and ask what Argentine bean they have (yes, there are local producers).

Specialty café

Cuervo Café

Thames, between Honduras and El Salvador Sat & Sun · ≈ 9 AM – 7 PM

Another excellent specialty spot, more relaxed in vibe. Ideal for sitting outside on a Sunday morning with a flat white and watching Palermo wake up. Near Plaza Serrano.

French patisserie

L'Harmonie

Gorriti, between Thames and Serrano Sat & Sun · ≈ 9 AM – 8 PM

A French pâtisserie that takes the idea of transporting you very seriously. The croissants are among the best in the city — and that's saying something in a town obsessed with medialunas. Sundays can be a small wait, but worth it.

Walking, looking, getting lost
Street art · Palermo Soho

The hidden passages

Coronel Cabrera · Russell · Soria · Santa Rosa Anytime · best with afternoon light

Short cobblestone passages with stencils, climbing plants, murals, drawings. Not signposted tourist attractions — they're real residential alleys where street art lives alongside old architecture in a way only Buenos Aires really does. Pasaje Santa Rosa (off Plaza Serrano) is the most intervened, a collective canvas.

Park · Free

Jardín Botánico Carlos Thays

Av. Santa Fe 3951 Sat & Sun · 8 AM – 6 PM

People walk past and don't go in. Mistake. A 19th-century garden by the same landscape architect who designed the Bosques de Palermo — sculptures hidden among the vegetation, an Art Nouveau greenhouse out of a novel, silent paths, and an absurd number of resident cats. Walk slowly. Morning light is best.

Park · Free

El Rosedal & the Palermo Lakes

Av. Infanta Isabel All day · best before 10 AM or after 5 PM

You've heard of it. The real tip: don't go on a Sunday at noon — it'll be packed with families and vendors. Go early morning or late afternoon. At those hours it's silent, the light is golden, the lakes mirror the trees. The Greek bridge, rose pergolas, paths. Cross toward Av. Figueroa Alcorta for a quieter corner with rowboats and a great skyline view. That's the "wow, this is Buenos Aires" moment.

Museum

MALBA

Av. Figueroa Alcorta 3415 Sat & Sun · 12 – 8 PM

The Museo de Arte Latinoamericano. The contemporary building is worth the visit on its own. Permanent collection has essential Latin American works (Frida Kahlo, Diego Rivera, Xul Solar, Berni) and the temporary shows are usually excellent. An hour and a half is enough. Great design shop. Walking distance from the Rosedal.

Gallery

Gachi Prieto Galería

Uriarte 1373 · Palermo Soho Sat · usually 2 – 7 PM · Sun check

Contemporary Argentine art gallery, dynamic and well-curated. Supports younger artists, runs residencies. The shows rotate often and there's always something interesting up. Free entry, friendly, not intimidating — a great entry point if you want to see what's happening in Argentine art right now.

Gallery

HACHE Galería

Loyola 32 · Villa Crespo border Sat · Sundays typically closed

Contemporary art with a sharp curatorial eye, in a beautifully restored PH. 5 minutes' walk from Palermo. If you're in the area on a Saturday, worth a peek.

San Telmo

The neighborhood with the most layers of history. Cobblestone streets, colonial townhouses turned galleries and bars, wrought-iron balconies. Sundays are San Telmo's best day — with a caveat: the Defensa Street fair gets very crowded. Go before 11am, or duck into the side streets.

From Palermo · Uber 15–20 min
Market · 1897

Mercado de San Telmo

Defensa 960 Sat & Sun · ≈ 8 AM – 7 PM

An iron-frame market from 1897 (like Les Halles, but it survived). Inside: a labyrinth of stalls — historic greengrocers, butchers, a deli with extraordinary Argentine cheeses, plus specialty cafés, antique stands and a craft brewery. Not a tourist build-out — a real working market that happens to also have beautiful things. Walk the interior aisles slowly.

Architecture · Antiques

Pasaje de la Defensa

Defensa 1179 Sat & Sun

A 19th-century townhouse turned into a passage of antique dealers and artisans. The real reason to go is the interior architecture: the patio with its skylight, the galleries, the marble staircase. Walk in even if you're not buying.

Hidden gem · Museum/passage

Cassa Lepage — Pasaje Belgrano

Bolívar 373 · San Telmo / Monserrat Sat & Sun

One of the best-kept secrets of the old city. A restored 19th-century passage that today is part cultural space, site museum and boutique hotel. Under glass floors, real archaeological remains — inkpots, jars, daily objects from the 17th–19th centuries. From the rooftop you can see domes, murals and a native plant garden. Argentina's first-ever film shoot happened in this passage in 1900. Go up to the terrace if you can.

English bookstore

Walrus Books

Estados Unidos 617 Sat 11 AM – 8 PM · Sun 12 – 7 PM

For English readers. A tiny, bohemian shop full of second-hand finds in Anglo-American literature. The owner recommends with real, genuine passion. It feels like walking into a friend's library — a friend who lived in New York and came back with suitcases of books.

Streets to wander: beyond Defensa, walk Bolívar, Carlos Calvo, Estados Unidos, Humberto Primo. Quiet blocks, original cobblestones, old trees — that feeling of being inside a European city that decided to stay in South America.

Recoleta

The most French neighborhood in Buenos Aires. Haussmann-style buildings, enormous trees, wide sidewalks. Where the porteño bourgeoisie of the 19th century decided to live — and you can feel it on every block.

From Palermo · Walk 25–30 min · or Uber 5–8 min
Museum · Free

Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes

Av. del Libertador 1473 Sat & Sun 11 AM – 8 PM · Tue closed

Permanent collection: Goya, El Greco, Rodin, Monet, Degas, Renoir, Modigliani — plus one of the best 19th–20th century Argentine art collections anywhere. The building was a former water pumping station, so the spaces are large and luminous. An hour and a half is enough. The European Impressionism room is surprisingly strong for a Latin American museum.

Sculpture + architecture

Floralis Genérica & Faculty of Law

Just behind Bellas Artes Anytime

Exit the museum through the back and you're facing the giant metal flower that opens and closes with the sun, and the impressive brutalist Faculty of Law building. Big-sky, big-space moment.

Hidden palace · Retiro

Palacio Noel — Museo Fernández Blanco

Suipacha 1422 Sat & Sun 11 AM – 8 PM · Tue closed

The kind of place that makes you say "how does this exist and nobody mentioned it?" A 1920s neocolonial palace built by architect Martín Noel as his own home — Limeño balconies, baroque details, and Andalusian-inspired gardens that are a hidden oasis. The interior has viceregal Hispano-American art, but the real mind-blower is the garden: olives, orange trees, ombú, jacarandás, and a silence you wouldn't believe is blocks from 9 de Julio. Go.

Plaza · Sunday fair

Plaza Francia + Recoleta Cultural Center

Junín 1930 Sunday craft fair active

The Sunday craft fair here is prettier and less mobbed than San Telmo's. Next to it: the free Centro Cultural Recoleta (always has contemporary art exhibitions) and the 1732 Iglesia del Pilar with colonial cloisters almost no one visits.

Streets to walk: Alvear, Arroyo, Juncal between Ayacucho and Junín. First-class French architecture, embassies, mansions, wrought iron. Avenida Alvear is one of the most elegant streets in South America. Look up.

Chacarita

The neighborhood nobody tells you about — but creative porteños adopted in the last few years. It doesn't have Palermo's polish or San Telmo's monumental history. It has something better: authenticity in process. Galleries in old warehouses, artist studios with open doors, cafés that don't need followers to survive.

From Palermo · Uber 8–10 min · or 15–20 min walk
Contemporary art

Fundación Cazadores

Av. Del Campo 1452 Sat · Sun depends on show

Contemporary art space in an enormous warehouse. Strong shows, well-curated, Argentine and Latin American artists. Sometimes performances, film cycles, events. The reconverted industrial building is part of the experience. Free.

Top-tier gallery

Ruth Benzacar Galería

Loyola 32 / Juan B. Justo 7702 Sat · Sundays generally closed

The most important contemporary art gallery in Argentina. It moved from elegant Plaza San Martín to a warehouse in Chacarita — and that move tells you everything about where the scene is going. Represents artists like Leandro Erlich and Tomás Saraceno. If you're in town on a Saturday, don't miss it.

Art + community

Oihoy — Espacio para la Regeneración

Loyola 770 Check schedule

An art-meets-community project that runs out of a house. Workshops, shows, events. The kind of space that only exists in Buenos Aires and that helps you understand the cultural scene from the inside.

Wander zone

Dorrego & Jorge Newbery

Several blocks · Chacarita Best Saturday afternoon

Small galleries, nameless cafés that are actually great, independent design shops, natural-wine bars — all mixed with old greengrocers and corner stores. Nothing built for tourists. The real city living alongside the new one.

Villa Crespo

Palermo's neighbor that refuses to be Palermo. Jewish-immigrant heritage, tango lineage (Pugliese was born here), cobblestone streets, lifelong bodegones, leather outlets on Murillo street, and a growing wave of galleries, cafés and bars that landed here when Palermo rents got impossible.

From Palermo · 10–15 min walk · or Uber 5 min
Used bookstore

Aristipo Libros

Serrano 1394 Sat 10:30 AM – 7:30 PM · Sun 2:30 – 7:30 PM

Used-book shop specialized in literature, philosophy and social sciences. Owner Patricio Rago has spent 13 years rescuing books from fairs, warehouses, and the Corrientes street shops. A bookseller of the old school: he recommends, he talks, he forms readers. Small space, every shelf curated.

Indie bookstore · Villa Crespo border

Librería Verne

Loyola 565 Sat 11 AM – 8 PM · Sun check

Founded by cultural journalist Maximiliano Tomas. Sharp catalogue: Argentine and foreign fiction, essay, poetry. The recommendation table rotates and always surprises. Neighborhood energy with first-rate editorial criteria. About 10–12 minutes by car from Palermo Soho, or 25 walking if you want the air.

Between Scalabrini Ortiz and Malabia Mostly Mon–Sat

The leather outlets street. Not glamorous, but if you want a real Argentine leather jacket at a real price, this is where porteños actually shop. Factories with public showrooms — not tourist boutiques.

Colegiales

The best-kept secret on this list. Just north of Palermo Hollywood, across the train tracks. Quieter than its famous neighbors, full of low houses, tree-lined streets, and the occasional architectural gem.

From Palermo · 10–15 min walk from Palermo Hollywood · or Uber 5 min
Hidden architecture

Pasaje General Paz

Zapata 552 / Ciudad de la Paz 561 Always (residential street) · best by day

A 1925 passage that preserves the spirit of the early-immigration collective housing: three floors of apartments around an inner courtyard with plants, iron bridges, checkered tiles, and the feel of another century. You can glimpse the charm from outside. If a neighbor lets you peek into the interior patio, it's a small time-travel. Not a tourist attraction — people live here. Look with care.

Indie bookstore

Céspedes Libros

Álvarez Thomas 853 Sat · Sun check

Independent bookstore with thoughtful curation and cultural events. Worth a stop if you're in the neighborhood.

Belgrano R

Very few tourists make it here. Belgrano R (the "R" is for the Belgrano R train station) is a micro-neighborhood of curving streets — rare in BA's grid city — low houses with gardens, tree-lined boulevards, and a calm that doesn't feel like Buenos Aires at all. Like an English village grafted onto the city.

From Palermo · Uber 10–12 min
Pure walking

Melián · La Pampa · Echeverría

The curving streets of Belgrano R Anytime · daylight ideal

Wander these for half an hour. Low houses with gardens, gentle curves, mature trees. Take photos — this side of Buenos Aires almost no visitor sees.

— Build your day

Four Sunday routes.

Pick one. Each one is the right amount of walking, eating, and looking up for a single Sunday afternoon. Don't try to do them all.

01 ≈ 3–4 hours · all walking

Books, gardens & coffee

All in Palermo. Start around 9:30 am.

  1. Walk into the Jardín Botánico. Find the Art Nouveau greenhouse, the hidden sculptures, the cats. 30–40 min.
  2. Cross into the Bosques de Palermo / Rosedal. Slow walk by the lake. 40 min.
  3. Walk 15 min through tree-lined streets to LAB Café. Order a pour-over.
  4. 10 min walk to Libros del Pasaje. Half an hour in the shelves.
  5. 5 min to Eterna Cadencia. Patio at the back. (If hungry: L'Harmonie, 3 blocks.)
Total: 3–4 hours. Everything close. Everything on foot.
02 ≈ 3 hours · Uber + walking

San Telmo, deeper

Beat the fair crowds. Be there by 10 am.

  1. Uber from Palermo to Defensa & San Juan. About 15 min.
  2. Walk Defensa north. Look at the façades. Enter the Mercado de San Telmo, café inside.
  3. Continue on Bolívar to Cassa Lepage (Pasaje Belgrano). Up to the rooftop.
  4. Back to Defensa for Pasaje de la Defensa. Interior architecture.
  5. Wander Carlos Calvo, Estados Unidos, Humberto Primo. Stop at Walrus Books if you read English.
  6. Uber back to Palermo.
Total: 3 hours. The original city, before Palermo was anything.
03 ≈ 3 hours · walk or Uber

Recoleta elegance + a hidden museum

For the architecture obsessed.

  1. Uber to Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes. About 10 min. Or walk it — gorgeous 25–30 min route via Av. Las Heras.
  2. Tour the museum at your own pace. 1–1.5 hours. Free.
  3. Exit through the back. Floralis Genérica + Faculty of Law.
  4. Walk 15 min to Palacio Noel. The gardens are the point. 30 min.
  5. Walk Arroyo & Av. Alvear. Then end at Plaza Francia + Recoleta Cultural Center.
Total: 3 hours. The most elegant slice of South America.
04 ≈ 2 hours · Saturday best

Creative Chacarita

Better on a Saturday — many galleries close Sundays.

  1. Uber to Fundación Cazadores. About 8 min.
  2. See the show. Step back outside.
  3. Walk Loyola, Dorrego, Jorge Newbery. Open studios, graffiti, no-name cafés that are great.
  4. If open, stop at Librería Verne (Loyola 565).
  5. Let the neighborhood show you what it has. Don't search for anything.
Total: 2 hours. Find what's next, before it's known.

🎁 For everything else · rainy-day backup · forgotten essentials
Local wisdom

Things a local would tell you.

A few things that don't fit anywhere else — but that will make your week noticeably better.

⤴

Look up.

The architecture in this city is extraordinarily varied — Art Nouveau, Art Déco, brutalist, neoclassical, all mixed on the same block. Almost no one notices. You can.

◷

Argentines eat late.

Restaurants don't fill before 9 PM. Cafés are at their best mid-morning (10–11 AM) or merienda time (5–6 PM). Sundays open slowly — don't expect activity before 10 AM.

$

Spend well, not little.

Use what you have to spend better, not less: buy a book, order two cortados instead of one, leave a generous tip. The currency situation here changes constantly — but kindness travels well.

⚲

Walk Palermo.

Palermo Soho, Palermo Hollywood, Palermo Chico — different sub-zones, all walkable. For other neighborhoods, a 5–15 minute Uber drops you in a different world.

⊙

The slow Sunday is the point.

Porteños take Sundays seriously: it's a day for walking, eating well, doing nothing productive. Don't pack 10 things in — do 3 or 4, well, with pauses.

⚠

Common sense.

Palermo, Recoleta, San Telmo (by day), Chacarita (by day), Villa Crespo, Colegiales, Belgrano — all safe to walk. Be discreet with valuables and your phone, carry a passport copy, leave the original at the hotel.
